An Introduction to IPOs

Unedited

An IPO is a process through which a company registers itself in a stock exchange for the first time and puts its shares up for sale. The reasons for a company to go public are far and varied but they usually revolve around raising capital for some purpose. Issuing a share to the public allows a company to raise capital from public investors.

Companies choose underwriters who choose the rate at which a company is to be traded or the initial price of the share-based on a thorough evaluation of the companies financial documents, its size etc.

The Dutch East India Company can be credited with the first IPO or something that very closely resembled it. Since then IPOs have increased with different purposes in each decade. Tech IPOs saw an increase during the late 90s after the 2008 financial crisis IPOs took a backseat for a while but have started to come back in recent years.

The SEBI manages the IPO process in India. Companies intending to go public must register with the SEBI, which scrutanizes all its financial documents, balance sheets and undergoes audits before granting approval.

One approved the price and number of shares are fixed. There are two types of IPOs fixed price and book building. In the first, a price is fixed whereas in the second a price is bid on within a range of values.

After making shares public investors have to submit applications. Once it gets subscriptions it allots the shares. Then the stock is listed in the markets.

IPOs have both advantages and disadvantages. The obvious advantage is that being one of the first few shareholders you have the ability to reap from a lot of potential growth of the company.There is some statistical evidence that IPOs outperform generic stock indices. Imagine investing in Apple when it went public! That being said there are also the inherent risks in it as with any investment and also the possibility of a bad IPO like that if WeWork in 2019.
